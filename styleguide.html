Grouping content

Paragraphs

Paragraphs, <p>, are used to represent textual content. Two examples of this element are found below, which have been separated from this paragraph with the <hr>-element.

Geralt was always going to stand out, with his white hair and piercing eyes, his cynicism and lack of respect for authority… but he is far more than just a striking-looking man. He’s a witcher; his sorcerous powers, enhanced by elixirs and long training, have made him a brilliant fighter and a merciless assassin. Yet he is no ordinary murderer: his targets are the vile fiends that ravage the land. But first appearances are often deceptive. Not everything monstrous-looking is evil, and not everything fair is good… and in every fairy tale, there is a grain of truth.

As a guardian of the innocent, Geralt, the witcher from Rivia, meets incestuous kings with undead daughters, vengeful djinns, shrieking harpies, lovelorn vampires and despondent ghouls: many are pernicious, some are wicked, and none are quite as they appear…

Blockquotes

Paragraphs that are quoted from external sources can also be presented as quotes with the <blockquote>-element.

Lesser, greater, middling, it's all the same. Proportions are negotiated, boundaries blurred. I'm not a pious hermit, I haven't done only good in my life. But if I'm to choose between one evil and another, then I prefer not to choose at all.

Lists

Lists can be represented in two different ways depending on their content.

Ordered lists

These lists, <ol>, and their direct children, <li>, are used to present ordered content, e.g. not-so-arbitrary rankings of people in the Witcher-universe.

  1. Sorceresses
    1. Vengerberg, Yennefer
    2. Merigold, Triss
    3. Eilhart, Philippa
    4. Vigo, Fringilla
    5. Metz, Keira
  2. Witchers
    1. Cirilla
    2. Geralt
    3. Eskel
    4. Lambert
    5. Vesemir

Unordered lists

These lists, <ul>, and their direct children, <li>, are used to present unordered content, i.e. lists where the order is unimportant; e.g. lists to use for navigational information, or really any kind of list-based content.

  • Nilfgaardian Empire
    • Cintra
    • Ebbing
    • Metinna
    • Nilfgaard
  • Redania
    • Drakenborg
    • Oxenfurt
    • Tretogor

Definition lists

An extra step beyond “regular” lists are definitions lists, <dl>, where terms, <dt>, are described by one or more definitions, <dd>.

Aen Ithlinnespeath
A prediction made by the elven prophetess Ithlinne. It is mentioned over the course of the Witcher series, and it is tied to the computer games rather prominently.
Witcher
An individual who has undergone extensive training and mysterious rituals (often taking place at “witcher schools” such as Kaer Morhen) in preparation for life as an itinerant monster-slayer for hire.

Pre-formatted text

The <pre>-element is useful for representing textual content that is dependent on an established formatting, e.g. source code you wish to share with others.

require 'sinatra'

class Frank << Sinatra::Base
  get '/' do
    'Hello, world!'
  end
end

Tabular elements

Tabular elements, which are listed below, are useful for presenting tabular data, i.e. information that is two-dimensional and representable by columns and rows.

  • <table>, the parent element for all tabular elements
  • <thead>, the element representing the table's header
  • <tfoot>, the element representing the table's footer
  • <tbody>, the element which contains all rows and their column values (cells)
  • <tr>, the row-element
  • <th>, the header-element for a specific column
  • <td>, the cell-element for a specific column

Media elements

Images

Images can be included with the <img>-element, where the <src>-attribute determines the origin of the image; the <alt>-attribute should always be used to assist in describing the image for accessibility purposes, unless it is used decoratively.

To present images – or other forms of media – in a contained way with accompanying elements, the <figure>-element can act as parent containing the image and other elements, e.g. <figcaption> for captions.

Text-level elements

Anchors

The anchor element, <a>, together with its href-attribute (which describes its destination), is used to create hyperlinks to web pages, files, and external applications.

Do me a favour and play The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt.

Emphasis

The emphasis element, <em>, is used to denote en emphasis on certain words or phrases within a sentence.

I was late to the series, but after playing a few hours of the third game, I knew I had to read the books, as well.

Strong importance

The strong element, <strong>, is used to denote importance or urgency within a sentence.

You absolutely must read the Witcher books, or at least play the third game.

Emboldening & italicising

The bold and italic elements, <b> and <i>, are used to embolden or italicise text without ascribing semantics to them (unlike the two previous elements).

Emboldening is often used to denote keywords in a summary or product names in an article, while italicising is used for technical terms, foreign language phrases, or fictional character thoughts.

The Witcher saga takes place in the aftermath of the first major war between the Nilfgaard Empire and the Northern Kingdoms, with a second war beginning in the middle of the series.

Geralt of Rivia, known also as Gwynnbleid (Elder Speech: “White Wolf”) and the Butcher of Blaviken, is the protagonist of the series and its adaptations.

Abbreviations

The abbreviation element, <abbr>, is used to represent abbreviations within text, often with the title-attribute, or more preferably, with inline text like in the second example below.

PHP is a general-purpose programming language originally designed for web development.

A CLI (command-line interface) is a useful tool for working more efficiently within various programming environments.

Definitions

The definition element, <dfn>, is used to represent definitions that may or may not need further descriptions.

A Witcher is an individual who has undergone extensive training and mysterious rituals in preparation for life as an itinerant monster-slayer for hire.

Citations

The citation element, <cite>, is used to reference creative works and must include the title of said work.

Eternity is hidden in every moment. – Lady of the Lake, by Andrzej Sapkowski.

Quotations

The quotation element, <q>, is used for including quotations of shorter lengths that do not require paragraph breaks.

The end justifies the means, Emhyr said dully; What I'm doing, I'm doing for posterity. To save the world.

If the world is to be saved like that…, the Witcher lifted his head, … it would be better for it to perish. Believe me, Duny, it'd be better if it perished.

Time references

The time element, <code>, is used to represent a specific time or period and is useful for interpretation of dates by search engines.

My birthday is January 2nd, 1992.

Inline code

The code element, <code>, is used to represents snippets of source code that is not large enough to require a pre-element, e.g. one-liners like a CLI-command.

If you have PHP installed, you can type php -S localhost:1234 into a CLI and start a simple server with a local folder as its root.

Keyboard prompts

The element for keyboard prompts, <kbd>, is useful when wanting to represent actionable keys for certain actions, e.g. shortcuts in a web application.

Use the W, A, S, and D-keys to control Geralt.

Variables

The variable element, <var>, is used to represent names of variables in a mathemical or programmatical environment.

In the special and general theories of relativity, c interrelates space and time, and also appears in the famous equation of mass–energy equivalence E = mc2.

Sub- and superscript

The sub- and superscript elements, <sub> and <sup>, are purely typographical elements and are used to denote stylistic standards, e.g. within chemical formulas.

The molecular formula for glucose is C6H12O6 (meaning that it is a molecule with 6 carbon atoms, 12 hydrogen atoms and 6 oxygen atoms).

Ordinal indicators are sometimes written as superscripts; 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 4th (rather than 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 4th).
